02 The Physics

The Magnus effect — nature's hidden force, weaponized for energy

In 1852, Heinrich Gustav Magnus discovered that a spinning cylinder in airflow experiences lateral force. The same physics curving a footballer's free kick can generate electricity from the lightest breeze.

↑ Live: spinning cylinder deflects airflow → low pressure above → LIFT force

How it works

A cylinder spins in moving air. One side accelerates airflow (low pressure), the other decelerates it (high pressure). The pressure difference creates lift — up to 14× the lift of a conventional airfoil.

Why not blades?

At 2–5 m/s, airfoil blades suffer laminar separation and stall. Spinning cylinders overcome this by injecting momentum into the boundary layer — producing 3× more power in low wind.

Battle-tested

Challenergy (Japan) commercially deploys Magnus VAWTs. Their unit survived Typhoon Chanthu — Category 5, gusts of 84 m/s. The technology is validated.
